Introduction to Natural Disasters

Earthquakes

What it is: A sudden and violent shaking of the ground, sometimes causing great destruction, as a result of movements within the earth's crust or volcanic action.

Causes: Earthquakes are usually caused when rock underground suddenly breaks along a fault. This sudden release of energy causes the seismic waves that make the ground shake.

Effects: The environmental effects of it are that including surface faulting, tectonic uplift and subsidence, tsunamis, soil liquefaction, ground resonance, landslides and ground failure, either directly linked to a quake source or provoked by the ground shaking.

Damage Costs: Between $500 million and $1.1 billion.

Ex: The deadliest earthquake in recorded history struck Shensi province in China in 1556, killing about 830,000 people.

Volcano Eruptions

What it is: A volcanic eruption occurs when hot materials from the earth's interior are thrown out of a volcano.

Causes: Volcanoes are formed by eruptions of lava and ash when magma rises through cracks or weak-spots in the Earth's crust. A build up of pressure in the earth is released, by things such as a plate movement which forces molten rock to exploded into the air causing a volcanic eruption.

Effects: They can cause rain, thunder and lightning. Volcanoes can also have long-term effects on the climate, making the world cooler. Fast-moving lava can kill people and falling ash can make it hard for them to breathe. They can also die from famine, fires and earthquakes which can be related to volcanoes.

Damage Costs: Planter's roads replacement costs are up to $10,700, Federal Aid Roads are up to $52,000, & Country roads replacement costs are up to $212,250

Ex: The 1815 eruption of Mount Tambora was one of the most powerful in recorded history, with a Volcanic Explosivity Index of 7, killing 92,000 people.

Drought

What it is: A natural disaster of below-average precipitation in a given region, resulting in prolonged shortages in the water supply, whether atmospheric, surface water or groundwater.

Causes: Land and water temperatures, air circulation and weather patterns, soil moisture levels, drought can also be a supply and demand of water issue.

Effects: Lower surface, lower flow levels, increased pollution of surface water, the drying out of wetlands, more and larger fires, losing biodiversity, worse health of trees and the appearance of pests.

Damage Costs: $9.5 billion per event.

Ex: The 2000s drought in Australia affected most of southern Australia, including its largest cities and largest agricultural region.

Floods

What it is: An overflow of water that submerges land that is usually dry.

Causes: Heavy rainfall, long periods of rain, snowmelt, steep slopes, impermeable rock (doesn't allow water through), very wet, saturated soils, compacted or dry soil.

Effects: Loss of human life, damage to property, destruction of crops, loss of livestock, and deterioration of health conditions owing to waterborne diseases.

Damage Costs: For 9-12 inches of flood, the estimate is $18,930. At 18 inches, the estimated cost is $26,285

Ex: The 1931 China floods is the deadliest natural disaster in history by estimated death toll (1,000,000–4,000,000).

Hurricanes

What it is: A storm with a violent wind, in particular a tropical cyclone in the Caribbean.

Causes: Hurricanes start when warm, moist air from the ocean surface begins to rise rapidly, where it encounters cooler air that causes the warm water vapor to condense and to form storm clouds and drops of rain.

Effects: Heavy rains, high winds, a storm surge, and even tornadoes.

Damage Costs: Between $50 billion and $161 billion.

Ex: The Great Hurricane of 1780 is the deadliest Atlantic hurricane in recorded history, which resulted killing 22,000–27,501 people.

Tornadoes

What it is: Rapidly rotating column of air that is in contact with both the surface of the Earth and a cumulonimbus cloud or, in rare cases, the base of a cumulus cloud.

Causes: Tornadoes form in unusually violent thunderstorms when there is sufficient instability and wind shear present in the lower atmosphere. Instability refers to unusually warm and humid conditions in the lower atmosphere, and possibly cooler than usual conditions in the upper atmosphere.

Effects: Massive destruction to homes, property, infrastructure and cause many deaths as well. Each year, about 60 people are killed by tornadoes, mostly from airborne debris.

Damage Costs: Tornadoes can cost up to $10 billion in the U.S.

Ex: The Daulatpur–Saturia tornado in Bangladesh on April 26, 1989, was the deadliest tornado in world history, which killed approximately 1,300 people.
